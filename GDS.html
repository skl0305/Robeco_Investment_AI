The China data center market is at a critical juncture in September 2025, characterized by a dichotomy: robust long-term demand drivers, particularly from hyperscale cloud providers and AI development, juxtaposed against a significant structural oversupply and evolving regulatory landscape. Technavio projects the market to grow by a staggering USD 274.39 billion from 2025-2029, at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 38.3%, largely driven by hyper-converged data centers and AI integration. Hyperscale giants like Alibaba and Tencent are indeed ramping up annual spending to exceed RMB 200 billion (US$28 billion) in 2025-2026 for computing power. However, consensus fails to fully integrate the counter-narrative of oversupply. Following a three-year construction boom, China is actively reassessing its data center strategy, with reports indicating up to 80% of new, state-backed computing capacity remains idle. This oversupply stems from unchecked expansion, encouraged by local officials eager to meet government-mandated AI leadership goals, resulting in over 500 projects announced and 150 completed by end-2024. The government is now building a national platform to sell this surplus compute power and curbing new, smaller local projects, signifying a market rationalization that will inevitably impact pricing power and new demand fulfillment across the industry, even for major players like GDS. This creates a long-term drag on industry-wide revenue per unit and capital efficiency, a second-order effect missed by many bullish forecasts. The massive increase in electricity use by data centers (projected +170% between 2024-2030 in China) also raises concerns about energy supply and sustainability, leading to government oversight.

Competitive Landscape: Intensifying Pressure Despite Leading Players' Scale Advantages

The competitive landscape within China's data center market, while featuring strong commitments from leading operators, is intensifying, creating a challenging environment for sustained margin expansion. GDS Holdings, alongside a few other major players, maintains a significant market position, with GDS reporting a 22% market share in the Chinese hyperscale data center market in 2023. These leading operators benefit from economies of scale and pre-committed capacity; GDS had commitment rates of 91.9% for area in service and 64.1% for area under construction in 2024. This suggests that while smaller, less competitive players are likely to bear the brunt of the oversupply, even market leaders will face indirect pressure. The increasing consolidation trend in the market, where major players expand and push smaller competitors to the margins, while potentially beneficial for market share, also implies a more aggressive pricing environment as dominant players vie for new hyperscale contracts. Furthermore, the geopolitical "chip war" between the US and China introduces a unique layer of complexity. US export controls on advanced AI chips from NVIDIA have prompted Chinese firms to cancel orders and seek domestic alternatives. While Huawei is rapidly advancing its Ascend AI processors, with new models expected in Q1 and Q4 2026, these still lag US chips by "one to two generations," creating a performance gap for the most demanding AI workloads. This means that while AI demand is real, the ability of Chinese data centers to deploy the most advanced, highest-value AI infrastructure is constrained, impacting the revenue per rack and specialized services segment. This competitive and technological dynamic will limit the true upside potential for premium AI data center services in China over the next 18-36 months.

Industry Investment Implications: Prudence Required Amidst Structural Shift and Valuation Risks

Our analysis indicates that investors need to adopt a prudent, contrarian approach to the China data center sector, particularly concerning companies like GDS. While the long-term demand for data centers, fueled by digitalization and AI, remains undeniable, the current market structure presents significant headwinds. The widespread oversupply of data center capacity, coupled with the government's intervention to rationalize the market, will depress pricing power and prolong the return on invested capital for new builds. This shifts the industry towards a more commoditized service, where sustained margin expansion becomes challenging, even for market leaders. Furthermore, the persistent geopolitical tensions around AI chip supply introduce a binary risk: either China develops truly competitive domestic chips quickly, or the high-performance AI segment remains constrained. Neither scenario guarantees outsized, profitable growth for operators like GDS. The market's current bullish sentiment, reflected in the "Strong Buy" consensus for GDS and high valuation multiples like its 19.88x EV/EBITDA, fundamentally misprices these structural risks and the erosion of recurring revenue through asset monetization [6, Metrics Grid]. Our sector allocation favors avoiding direct exposure to highly leveraged, capital-intensive Chinese infrastructure plays until there is clear evidence of market rationalization, sustained organic profitability (excluding one-off gains), and resolution of AI chip supply chain uncertainties. We believe the risk-reward profile is skewed to the downside, justifying an UNDERWEIGHT rating for GDS and similar companies within the sector.
